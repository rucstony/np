				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Probability_theory" title="Probability theory">probability theory</a>, the <b>Chernoff bound</b>, named after <a href="/wiki/Herman_Chernoff" title="Herman Chernoff">Herman Chernoff</a>, gives exponentially decreasing bounds on tail distributions of sums of independent random variables. It is a sharper bound than the known first or second moment based tail bounds such as <a href="/wiki/Markov%27s_inequality" title="Markov's inequality">Markov's inequality</a> or <a href="/wiki/Chebyshev_inequality" title="Chebyshev inequality" class="mw-redirect">Chebyshev inequality</a>, which only yield power-law bounds on tail decay. However, the Chernoff bound requires that the variates be independent - a condition that neither the Markov nor the Chebyshev inequalities require.</p>
<p>It is related to the (historically earliest) <a href="/wiki/Bernstein_inequalities_(probability_theory)" title="Bernstein inequalities (probability theory)">Bernstein inequalities</a>, and to <a href="/wiki/Hoeffding%27s_inequality" title="Hoeffding's inequality">Hoeffding's inequality</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">Definition</span></h2>
<p>Let <i>X</i><sub>1</sub>, ..., <i>X</i><sub><i>n</i></sub> be independent <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_random_variable" title="Bernoulli random variable" class="mw-redirect">Bernoulli random variables</a>, each having probability <i>p</i> &gt; 1/2. Then the probability of simultaneous occurrence of more than <i>n</i>/2 of the events <img class="tex" alt="\{X_k = 1\}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/2/3/c233e837b1761c600ae4d71491411e88.png" /> has an exact value <i>S</i>, where</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt=" S=\sum\limits_{i = \lfloor \frac{n}{2} \rfloor + 1}^n \binom{n}{i}p^i (1 - p)^{n - i} ." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/2/9/229e310a728794fa7b4d16ed5548aef3.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>The Chernoff bound shows that <i>S</i> has the following lower bound:</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt=" S \ge 1 - \mathrm{e}^{- 2n \left( {p - \frac{1}{2}} \right)^2} ." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/c/c/accc024fe102e3e838fcc4abfa474577.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>This result admits various generalizations as outlined below. One can encounter many flavours of Chernoff bounds: the original <i>additive form</i> (which gives a bound on the <a href="/wiki/Approximation_error" title="Approximation error">absolute error</a>) or the more practical <i>multiplicative form</i> (which bounds the <a href="/wiki/Approximation_error" title="Approximation error">error relative</a> to the mean).</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: A motivating example">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="A_motivating_example">A motivating example</span></h2>
<div class="floatright"><a href="/wiki/File:Chernoff_bound.png" class="image"><img alt="Chernoff bound.png" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/e/ea/Chernoff_bound.png" width="425" height="560" /></a></div>
<p>The simplest case of Chernoff bounds is used to bound the success probability of majority agreement for n independent, equally likely events.</p>
<p>A simple motivating example is to consider a biased coin. One side (say, Heads), is more likely to come up than the other, but you don't know which and would like to find out. The obvious solution is to flip it many times and then choose the side that comes up the most. But how many times do you have to flip it to be confident that you've chosen correctly?</p>
<p>In our example, let <img class="tex" alt="X_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/f/c/4fc3e3c98d13ed389817f11dc66c10a6.png" /> denote the event that the <i>i</i>th coin flip comes up Heads; suppose that we want to ensure we choose the wrong side with at most a small probability <i>ε</i>. Then, rearranging the above, we must have:</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt=" n \geq \frac{1}{(p -1/2)^2} \ln \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon}}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/e/8/ee8a9210ce824b319da54e53fc623343.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>If the coin is noticeably biased, say coming up on one side 60% of the time (<i>p</i> = .6), then we can guess that side with 95% (<img class="tex" alt="\epsilon = .05" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/7/e/d7e2c354d15474dca372b92650e23942.png" />) accuracy after 150 flips<img class="tex" alt="(n=150)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/5/0/450186eb4d2adcde5b7ec21e63b9d916.png" />. If it is 90% biased, then a mere 10 flips suffices. If the coin is only biased a tiny amount, like most real coins are, the number of necessary flips becomes much larger.</p>
<p>More practically, the Chernoff bound is used in <a href="/wiki/Randomized_algorithm" title="Randomized algorithm">randomized algorithms</a> (or in computational devices such as <a href="/wiki/Quantum_computer" title="Quantum computer">quantum computers</a>) to determine a bound on the number of runs necessary to determine a value by majority agreement, up to a specified probability. For example, suppose an algorithm (or machine) <i>A</i> computes the correct value of a function <i>f</i> with probability <i>p</i> &gt; 1/2. If we choose <i>n</i> satisfying the inequality above, the probability that a majority exists and is equal to the correct value is at least 1 − ε, which for small enough ε is quite reliable. If <i>p</i> is a constant, ε diminishes exponentially with growing <i>n</i>, which is what makes algorithms in the complexity class <a href="/wiki/BPP_(complexity)" title="BPP (complexity)">BPP</a> efficient.</p>
<p>Notice that if <i>p</i> is very close to 1/2, the necessary <i>n</i> can become very large. For example, if <i>p</i> = 1/2 + 1/2<sup><i>m</i></sup>, as it might be in some <a href="/wiki/PP_(complexity)" title="PP (complexity)">PP</a> algorithms, the result is that <i>n</i> is bounded below by an exponential function in <i>m</i>:</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt=" n \geq 2^{2m} \ln \frac{1}{\sqrt{\varepsilon}}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/4/d/54d03960574ee782c68f90bebc6ffa0d.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The first step in the proof of Chernoff bounds">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="The_first_step_in_the_proof_of_Chernoff_bounds">The first step in the proof of Chernoff bounds</span></h2>
<p>The Chernoff bound for a random variable <i>X</i>, which is the sum of n independent random variables <img class="tex" alt="X_1, X_2, ..., X_n" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/e/c/4ec346f36f8e44fc798228b44dd2a196.png" />, is obtained by applying <i>e</i><sup><i>tX</i></sup> for some well-chosen value of <i>t</i>. This method was first applied by <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Bernstein" title="Sergei Bernstein" class="mw-redirect">Sergei Bernstein</a> to prove the related <a href="/wiki/Bernstein_inequalities_(probability_theory)" title="Bernstein inequalities (probability theory)">Bernstein inequalities</a>.</p>
<p>From <a href="/wiki/Markov%27s_inequality" title="Markov's inequality">Markov's inequality</a> and using independence we can derive the following useful inequality:</p>
<p>For any <i>t</i> &gt; 0,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr\left[X \ge a\right] = \Pr\left[e^{tX} \ge e^{ta}\right] \le \frac{ E[e^{tX}]}{e^{ta}} = {\prod_i E[e^{tX_i}]\over e^{ta}}." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/9/7/0971519306bef0bdad94de5992ff3ce3.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>In particular optimizing over t and using independence we obtain,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt=" \Pr\left[X \ge a\right]  \leq \min_{t&gt;0} {\prod_i E[e^{tX_i}] \over e^{ta}}. \quad (+) " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/8/c/68c39de747bf6679a9065b365d762f5f.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>Similarly,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr\left[X \le a\right] = \Pr\left[e^{-tX} \ge e^{-ta}\right] " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/a/4bae3d1db62d5384e5d9c4329cda7aa7.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>and so,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt=" \Pr\left[X \le a\right] \leq \min_{t&gt;0} e^{ta} \prod_i E[e^{-tX_i}]  .  \quad (++) " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/2/2/92219af464d96fce127c5a2767e3cd76.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Precise statements and proofs">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Precise_statements_and_proofs">Precise statements and proofs</span></h2>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Theorem for additive form (absolute error)">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Theorem_for_additive_form_.28absolute_error.29">Theorem for additive form (absolute error)</span></h3>
<p>The following Theorem is due to <a href="/wiki/Wassily_Hoeffding" title="Wassily Hoeffding">Wassily Hoeffding</a> and hence is called Chernoff-Hoeffding theorem.</p>
<p>Assume random variables <img class="tex" alt="X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_m" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/5/6/35625821b13b59a90ed21c46b49149bc.png" /> are <a href="/wiki/I.i.d." title="I.i.d." class="mw-redirect">i.i.d.</a> Let <img class="tex" alt="p = E \left [X_i \right ]" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/5/e/65e8f48e9c48a7831e863575eef6ddd0.png" />, <img class="tex" alt="X_i \in \{0,1\}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/e/d/8edffde45c1c167ecd302a3860ef662c.png" />, and <img class="tex" alt="\varepsilon &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/0/f/b0f19c5714fe9f9891ed26ff783cf639.png" />. Then</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\begin{align}
&amp;\Pr\left[ \frac 1 m \sum X_i \geq p + \varepsilon \right] \\
&amp;\qquad\leq \left ( {\left (\frac{p}{p + \varepsilon}\right )}^{p+\varepsilon} {\left (\frac{1 - p}{1 -p - \varepsilon}\right )}^{1 - p- \varepsilon}\right ) ^m =  e^{ - D(p+\varepsilon\|p) m}
\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/7/9/9793d242cfdd24b255ac9e7a78dbf304.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>and</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\begin{align}
&amp;\Pr\left[ \frac 1 m \sum X_i \leq p - \varepsilon \right] \\
&amp;\qquad\leq \left ( {\left (\frac{p}{p - \varepsilon}\right )}^{p-\varepsilon} {\left (\frac{1 - p}{1 -p + \varepsilon}\right )}^{1 - p+ \varepsilon}\right ) ^m = e^{ - D(p-\varepsilon\|p) m},
\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/5/1/351197aef50bc71b58cb8060f43f5962.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>where</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
 D(x||y) = x \log \frac{x}{y} + (1-x) \log \frac{1-x}{1-y}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/4/4/f44679ba90c278a64fd2ef74c873faf0.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>is the <a href="/wiki/Kullback-Leibler_divergence" title="Kullback-Leibler divergence" class="mw-redirect">Kullback-Leibler divergence</a> between <a href="/wiki/Bernoulli_distribution" title="Bernoulli distribution">Bernoulli distributed</a> random variables with parameters <img class="tex" alt="x" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/d/9dd4e461268c8034f5c8564e155c67a6.png" /> and <img class="tex" alt="y" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/1/5/415290769594460e2e485922904f345d.png" /> respectively. If <img class="tex" alt=" p\geq 1/2 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/6/e/76e3543d33995bd70c0a9ed7c2b99963.png" />, then <img class="tex" alt=" \Pr\left[ X&gt;mp+x \right] \leq \exp(-x^2/2mp(1-p)) ." src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/7/6976d89bd429a29f9bafeb63e5828b6c.png" /></p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Proof">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Proof">Proof</span></h4>
<p>The proof starts from the general inequality (+) above. <img class="tex" alt="q = p + \varepsilon" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/2/2/3225349d7c1fe4612dc64b9193b72169.png" />. Taking <i>a = mq</i> in (+), we obtain:</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\Pr\left[ \frac{1}{m} \sum X_i \ge q\right] \le \inf_{t&gt;0} \frac{E \left[\prod e^{t X_i}\right]}{e^{tmq}}
= \inf_{t&gt;0} \left[\frac{ E\left[e^{tX_i} \right] }{e^{tq}}\right]^m .
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/2/9/229d15bdd41dae46e3adfd6689b66fe6.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Now, knowing that <img class="tex" alt="\Pr[X_i = 1] = p" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/7/5/07572387fa1a159f3dc2807c165596f5.png" />, <img class="tex" alt="\Pr[X_i = 0] = (1-p)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/1/0/710bb7f75b6461c7a9f8cc0ee6075e38.png" />, we have</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\left[\frac{ E\left[e^{tX_i} \right] }{e^{tq}}\right]^m
= \left[\frac{p e^t + (1-p)}{e^{tq} }\right]^m
= [pe^{(1-q)t} + (1-p)e^{-qt}]^m.
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/5/8253e790764153366a0b9505d8154a4a.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Therefore we can easily compute the infimum, using calculus and some logarithms. Thus,</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\begin{align}
&amp;\frac{d}{dt} \log(pe^{(1-q)t} + (1-p)e^{-qt}) \\
&amp;\qquad=
\frac{1}{pe^{(1-q)t} + (1-p)e^{-qt}}
((1-q)pe^{(1-q)t}-q(1-p)e^{-qt}) \\
&amp;\qquad = -q + \frac{pe^{(1-q)t}}{pe^{(1-q)t}+(1-p)e^{-qt}}
\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/a/b/5ab28557cfc6faf47605009052087e4b.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>Setting the last equation to zero and solving, we have</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\begin{align}
q &amp; = \frac{pe^{(1-q)t}}{pe^{(1-q)t}+(1-p)e^{-qt}} =
\frac{pe^{(1-q)t}}{e^{-qt}(pe^{t}+(1-p))} \\
pe^{(1-q)t} &amp; = pe^{-qt}e^t = qe^{-qt}(pe^{t}+1-p) \\
\frac{p}{q}e^t &amp; = pe^t + 1-p 
\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/5/d/55d573c7691ba01428558f7fc82121f7.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>so that <img class="tex" alt="e^t = (1-p)\left(\frac{p}{q}-p\right)^{-1}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/d/9/7d9cc3ce2ead4b9a620488f1ed47e9a2.png" />.</p>
<p>Thus, <img class="tex" alt="t = \log\left(\frac{(1-p)q}{(1-q)p}\right)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/b/7/eb7bb152f225a2cd9d4ecb5f629cb13c.png" />.</p>
<p>As <img class="tex" alt="q = p+\varepsilon &gt; p" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/5/3/6534178ea0a9daf840ee378d6623a5b5.png" />, we see that <img class="tex" alt="t &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/3/1/231b8bbeb4fa45885c97d90d3d2637be.png" />, so our bound is satisfied on <img class="tex" alt="t" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png" />. Having solved for <img class="tex" alt="t" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png" />, we can plug back into the equations above to find that</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\begin{align}
&amp;\log(pe^{(1-q)t} + (1-p)e^{-qt}) = \log[e^{-qt}(1-p+pe^t)] \\
&amp;\qquad = \log\left[e^{-q \log\left(\frac{(1-p)q}{(1-q)p}\right)}\right] + 
\log\left[1-p+pe^{\log\left(\frac{1-p}{1-q}\right)}e^{\log\frac{q}{p}}\right] \\
&amp;\qquad = -q\log\frac{1-p}{1-q} -q \log\frac{q}{p} + \log\left[1-p+ p\left(\frac{1-p}{1-q}\right)\frac{q}{p}\right] \\
&amp;\qquad = -q\log\frac{1-p}{1-q} -q \log\frac{q}{p} + \log\left[\frac{(1-p)(1-q)}{1-q}+\frac{(1-p)q}{1-q}\right] \\
&amp;\qquad = -q\log\frac{q}{p} + (1-q)\log\frac{1-p}{1-q} = -D(q \| p).
\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/7/c/f7c1c492a44353a9e0951de5e3c2da27.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>We now have our desired result, that</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\Pr\left[\frac{1}{m}\sum X_i \ge p + \varepsilon\right] \le e^{-D(p+\varepsilon\|p) m}.
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/6/5/765370d4625926e193b4ba4be8ae9ffb.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>To complete the proof for the symmetric case, we simply define the random variable <img class="tex" alt="Y_i = 1-X_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/c/6/ec6c65ac88a1bf7312cc2d3e77452a15.png" />, apply the same proof, and plug it into our bound.</p>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Simpler bounds">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Simpler_bounds">Simpler bounds</span></h4>
<p>A simpler bound follows by relaxing the theorem using <img class="tex" alt="D( p + x \| p) \geq 2 x^2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/c/5/9c537ce929e1bfe29f9e87d562219a27.png" />, which follows from the <a href="/wiki/Convex_function" title="Convex function">convexity</a> of <img class="tex" alt="D(p+x\| p)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/b/2/fb23dd511d05125fb79670e3c841903b.png" /> and the fact that <img class="tex" alt="\frac{d^2}{dx^2} D(p+x\|p) = \frac{1}{(p+x)(1-p-x)}\geq 4=\frac{d^2}{dx^2}(2x^2)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/1/2/112e55f1801cd76822ad9c14ff2b7d73.png" />. This results in a special case of <a href="/wiki/Hoeffding%27s_inequality" title="Hoeffding's inequality">Hoeffding's inequality</a>. Sometimes, the bound <img class="tex" alt="D( (1+x) p \| p) \geq x^2 p/4" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/6/d/36ddb0a6e3c89aed4cb84d201b9a5ec8.png" /> for <img class="tex" alt="-1/2 \leq x \leq 1/2" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/f/4/4f4fa8c8d1525fb9059ff6ff9f0f8948.png" />, which is stronger for <img class="tex" alt="p&lt;1/8" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/1/a/31a3e326ad674afa2abd4acea346b859.png" />, is also used.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Theorem for multiplicative form of Chernoff bound (relative error)">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Theorem_for_multiplicative_form_of_Chernoff_bound_.28relative_error.29">Theorem for multiplicative form of Chernoff bound (relative error)</span></h3>
<p>Let random variables <img class="tex" alt="X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/a/a/3aaa82d91860d83b245cb541ab06ba83.png" /> be <a href="/wiki/Statistical_independence" title="Statistical independence" class="mw-redirect">independent</a> random variables taking on values 0 or 1. Further, assume that <img class="tex" alt="\Pr(X_i = 1) = p_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/f/1/b/f1b929a7f6b6a0cd90fda603f99d06df.png" />. Then, if we let <img class="tex" alt="X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/c/3/ac355d51e0f744248539e4b3c62646ff.png" /> and <img class="tex" alt="\mu" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/7/2/b72bb92668acc30b4474caff40274044.png" /> be the expectation of <img class="tex" alt="X" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/2/1/02129bb861061d1a052c592e2dc6b383.png" />, for any <img class="tex" alt="\delta &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/c/b/1cb24dafc8035d2c720256620066ae73.png" /></p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\Pr \left[ X &gt; (1+\delta)\mu\right] &lt; \left(\frac{e^\delta}{(1+\delta)^{(1+\delta)}}\right)^\mu.
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/c/6/cc6c1bc14b614b143bde62264eef9285.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<h4><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Proof">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Proof_2">Proof</span></h4>
<p>According to (+),</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\begin{align}
\Pr[X &gt; (1 + \delta)\mu)] &amp; \le \inf_{t &gt; 0} \frac{\mathbf{E}\left[\prod_{i=1}^n\exp(tX_i)\right]}{\exp(t(1+\delta)\mu)} \\
&amp; = \inf_{t &gt; 0} \frac{\prod_{i=1}^n\mathbf{E}[\exp(tX_i)]}{\exp(t(1+\delta)\mu)} \\
&amp; = \inf_{t &gt; 0} \frac{\prod_{i=1}^n\left[p_i\exp(t) + (1-p_i)\right]}{\exp(t(1+\delta)\mu)}
\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/c/5/5c55bef6d6dd76ac309dd1e0cf82be9c.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The third line above follows because <img class="tex" alt="e^{tX_i}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/1/6/a16d9c310a3afb75eac78eccfe2d5b54.png" /> takes the value <img class="tex" alt="e^{t}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/d/c/9dc17cfb1082b61116b10632b774b463.png" /> with probability <img class="tex" alt="p_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/a/4/8a4bbd153c74655abb7ca04c0fa901d8.png" /> and the value <img class="tex" alt="1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/4/c/c4ca4238a0b923820dcc509a6f75849b.png" /> with probability <img class="tex" alt="1-p_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/5/0/950f323dffe1150a508a5ef9ea77db8b.png" />. This is identical to the calculation above in the proof of the <a href="#Theorem_for_additive_form_.28absolute_error.29">Theorem for additive form (absolute error)</a>.</p>
<p>Rewriting <img class="tex" alt="p_ie^t + (1-p_i)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/3/c/33c81eb98539b8fb785716a3277b7933.png" /> as <img class="tex" alt="p_i(e^t-1) + 1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/8/b/d8b55e9ecd7a338fc871dcde81dcf50b.png" /> and recalling that <img class="tex" alt="1+x \le e^x" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/d/f/adfbce6ef55e823cc50ae76e8250820c.png" /> (with strict inequality if <img class="tex" alt="x &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/8/7/887fb68a10cbd4369b27c90bee0334d8.png" />), we set <img class="tex" alt="x = p_i(e^t-1)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/a/e/0ae09a989ef53a56fe6c0cde9aeba4b0.png" />. The same result can be obtained by directly replacing <i>a</i> in the equation for the Chernoff bound with <img class="tex" alt="(1+\delta)\mu" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/a/6/6a6a4c51ea778b213bd06a87f0965c60.png" />.<sup id="cite_ref-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-0"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Thus,</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\begin{align}
&amp;\Pr[X &gt; (1+\delta)\mu] &lt; \frac{\prod_{i=1}^n\exp(p_i(e^t-1))}{\exp(t(1+\delta)\mu)} \\
&amp;\qquad =
\frac{\exp\left((e^t-1)\sum_{i=1}^n p_i\right)}{\exp(t(1+\delta)\mu)} 
= \frac{\exp((e^t-1)\mu)}{\exp(t(1+\delta)\mu)}.
\end{align}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/3/8/938ff25f1a0cff33bd2232dbff865ef6.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>If we simply set <img class="tex" alt="t = \log(1+\delta)" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/4/1/0411704eed61174041015678c75d7f5a.png" /> so that <img class="tex" alt=" t &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/2/3/1/231b8bbeb4fa45885c97d90d3d2637be.png" /> for <img class="tex" alt="\delta &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/c/b/1cb24dafc8035d2c720256620066ae73.png" />, we can substitute and find</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\frac{\exp((e^t-1)\mu)}{\exp(t(1+\delta)\mu)} = 
\frac{\exp((1+\delta - 1)\mu)}{(1+\delta)^{(1+\delta)\mu}} =
\left[\frac{\exp(\delta)}{(1+\delta)^{(1+\delta)}}\right]^\mu
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/9/0/1/901e0a65d4e596a7608adf3b1d141cae.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>This proves the result desired. A similar proof strategy can be used to show that</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\Pr[X &lt; (1-\delta)\mu] &lt; \exp(-\mu\delta^2/2).
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/7/8/b78466366df8a0f4efaaca5942c0cfcc.png" /></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Better Chernoff bounds for some special cases">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Better_Chernoff_bounds_for_some_special_cases">Better Chernoff bounds for some special cases</span></h3>
<p>We can obtain stronger bounds using simpler proof techniques for some special cases of symmetric random variables.</p>
<p>Let <img class="tex" alt="X_1, X_2, ..., X_n" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/e/c/4ec346f36f8e44fc798228b44dd2a196.png" /> be independent random variables,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/c/3/ac355d51e0f744248539e4b3c62646ff.png" />.</dd>
</dl>
<p>(a) <img class="tex" alt="\Pr(X_i = 1)  = \Pr(X_i = -1) = \frac{1}{2}" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/b/b/0bb103df8a0045f441f15aeaec918082.png" />.</p>
<p>Then,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr( X \ge a) \le e^{\frac{-a^2}{2n}}, \quad a &gt; 0 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/a/f/3af7e49fdc7e702708eb3b992c3317eb.png" />,</dd>
</dl>
<p>and therefore also</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr( |X| \ge a) \le 2e^{\frac{-a^2}{2n}}, \quad a &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/a/1/3a19953927cb89dc07eb869340a88577.png" /> .</dd>
</dl>
<p>(b) <img class="tex" alt="\Pr(X_i = 1)  = \Pr(X_i = 0) = \frac{1}{2}, \mathbf{E}[X] = \mu = \frac{n}{2} " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/8/1/68163779a5d3278dc271f8e0493ed9c3.png" /></p>
<p>Then,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr( X \ge \mu+a) \le e^{\frac{-2a^2}{n}}, \quad a &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/b/c/cbc8a0fd5bc468f708551ce58c82250e.png" />,</dd>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr( X \ge (1+\delta)\mu) \le e^{-\frac{\delta^2\mu}{3}}, \quad \delta &gt; 0" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/2/a/c2a8d6dbe425ab856ee508a37d370a45.png" />,</dd>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr( X \le \mu-a) \le e^{\frac{-2a^2}{n}}, \quad 0 &lt; a &lt; \mu" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/4/5/d453a7abee0fc8c212ba36e1799959be.png" />,</dd>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="\Pr( X \le (1-\delta)\mu) \le e^{-\frac{\delta^2\mu}{2}}, \quad 0 &lt; \delta &lt; 1" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/1/d/c1d11c908dab2d418346da3c1df0b571.png" />.</dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Applications of Chernoff bound">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications_of_Chernoff_bound">Applications of Chernoff bound</span></h2>
<p>Chernoff bounds have very useful applications in <a href="/w/index.php?title=Set_balancing&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Set balancing (page does not exist)">set balancing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Packet_(information_technology)" title="Packet (information technology)" class="mw-redirect">packet</a> <a href="/wiki/Routing" title="Routing">routing</a> in <a href="/wiki/Sparse_graph" title="Sparse graph" class="mw-redirect">sparse</a> networks.</p>
<p>The set balancing problem arises while designing statistical experiments. Typically while designing a statistical experiment, given the features of each participant in the experiment, we need to know how to divide the participants into 2 disjoint groups such that each feature is roughly as balanced as possible between the two groups. Refer to this <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=0bAYl6d7hvkC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;source=gbs_summary_r&amp;cad=0#PPA71,M1">book section</a> for more info on the problem.</p>
<p>Chernoff bounds are also used to obtain tight bounds for permutation routing problems which reduce <a href="/wiki/Network_congestion" title="Network congestion">network congestion</a> while routing packets in sparse networks. Refer to this <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://books.google.com/books?id=0bAYl6d7hvkC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;source=gbs_summary_r&amp;cad=0#PPA72,M1">book section</a> for a thorough treatment of the problem.</p>
<h2><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Matrix Chernoff bound">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Matrix_Chernoff_bound">Matrix Chernoff bound</span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Ahlswede" title="Rudolf Ahlswede">Rudolf Ahlswede</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andreas_Winter" title="Andreas Winter">Andreas Winter</a> introduced (<a href="#CITEREFAhlswedeWinter2003">Ahlswede &amp; Winter 2003</a>) a Chernoff bound for matrix-valued random variables.</p>
<p>If <img class="tex" alt=" M " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/6/69691c7bdcc3ce6d5d8a1361f22d04ac.png" /> is distributed according to some distribution over <img class="tex" alt=" d \times d " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/c/0/d/c0d8b4b800fcd5f789307e7b57bd6c2f.png" /> matrices with zero mean, and if <img class="tex" alt=" M_1,M_2,\ldots,M_t " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/e/4/5e44175d94f089b5f80cf02be46d0ccf.png" /> are independent copies of <img class="tex" alt="M" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/6/69691c7bdcc3ce6d5d8a1361f22d04ac.png" /> then for any <img class="tex" alt=" \varepsilon &gt; 0 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/b/0/f/b0f19c5714fe9f9891ed26ff783cf639.png" />,</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\Pr \left( \bigg\Vert \frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^t M_i - \mathbf{E}[M] \bigg\Vert_2 &gt; \varepsilon \right) \leq d \exp \left( -C \frac{\varepsilon^2 t}{\gamma^2} \right).
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/2/2/422e91b10caad11d4e8f3af28f7e72f1.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>where <img class="tex" alt=" \lVert M \rVert_2 \leq \gamma " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/7/5/8/758ec2af85df2833fdb13016e7fed514.png" /> holds almost surely and <img class="tex" alt=" C&gt;0 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/d/5/d/d5d86d712b9871e01d3a8ec699221864.png" /> is an absolute constant.</p>
<p>Notice that the number of samples in the inequality depends logarithmically on <img class="tex" alt="d" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/7/8277e0910d750195b448797616e091ad.png" />. In general, unfortunately, such a dependency is inevitable: take for example a diagonal random sign matrix of dimension <img class="tex" alt="d" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/7/8277e0910d750195b448797616e091ad.png" />. The operator norm of the sum of <img class="tex" alt="t" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png" /> independent samples is precisely the maximum deviation among <img class="tex" alt="d" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/7/8277e0910d750195b448797616e091ad.png" /> independent random walks of length <img class="tex" alt="t" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png" />. In order to achieve a fixed bound on the maximum deviation with constant probability, it is easy to see that <img class="tex" alt="t" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/e/3/5/e358efa489f58062f10dd7316b65649e.png" /> should grow logarithmically with <img class="tex" alt="d" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/8/2/7/8277e0910d750195b448797616e091ad.png" /> in this scenario.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The following theorem can be obtained by assuming <img class="tex" alt="M" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/6/69691c7bdcc3ce6d5d8a1361f22d04ac.png" /> has low rank, in order to avoid the dependency on the dimensions.</p>
<h3><span class="editsection">[<a href="/w/index.php?title=Chernoff_bound&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Theorem without the dependency on the dimensions">edit</a>]</span> <span class="mw-headline" id="Theorem_without_the_dependency_on_the_dimensions">Theorem without the dependency on the dimensions</span></h3>
<p>Let <img class="tex" alt=" 0&lt;\varepsilon&lt;1 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/f/8/af8ea28aa3b4391224e748a34eed2646.png" /> and <img class="tex" alt=" M " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/6/69691c7bdcc3ce6d5d8a1361f22d04ac.png" /> be a random symmetric real matrix with <img class="tex" alt=" \Vert \mathbf{E}[M] \Vert_2 \leq 1 " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/1/b/4/1b470987749db4eeec17b453f4c36e4f.png" /> and <img class="tex" alt=" \Vert M \Vert_2 \leq \gamma " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/5/0/45023471654ab439b88c2d97d56b2063.png" /> almost surely. Assume that each element on the support of <img class="tex" alt=" M " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/6/69691c7bdcc3ce6d5d8a1361f22d04ac.png" /> has at most rank <img class="tex" alt=" r " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/4/b/4/4b43b0aee35624cd95b910189b3dc231.png" />. Set</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt=" 
t = \Omega \left( \frac{\gamma\log (\gamma/\varepsilon^2)}{\varepsilon^2} \right)
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/a/9/5/a95f9a55bcac6e7c274f7fd7f98771ce.png" />.</dd>
</dl>
<p>If <img class="tex" alt=" r \leq t " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/0/a/7/0a7e2f101598ce26e3bf1a87e8a0a2f4.png" /> holds almost surely, then</p>
<dl>
<dd><img class="tex" alt="
\Pr \left( \bigg\Vert \frac{1}{t} \sum_{i=1}^t M_i - \mathbf{E}[M] \bigg\Vert_2 &gt; \varepsilon \right) \leq \frac{1}{\mathbf{poly}(t)}
" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/3/7/3/37300d50c88e67fdd53e24f71d164896.png" /></dd>
</dl>
<p>where <img class="tex" alt=" M_1,M_2,\ldots,M_t " src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/5/e/4/5e44175d94f089b5f80cf02be46d0ccf.png" /> are i.i.d. copies of <img class="tex" alt="M" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/math/6/9/6/69691c7bdcc3ce6d5d8a1361f22d04ac.png" />.</p>
